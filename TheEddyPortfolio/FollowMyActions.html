As a father I learned very quickly that my son could care less about what I say--he’s going to emulate what I do. I’ve been known to give a few speeches to ‘rally the troops’, but my true call to action for my peers and those under my guidance is simply for them to watch what I do and follow suit. I will show you what I need you to do.

My wedding vows were along the lines of as follows:

“I am a wordsmith by trade. I speak well for a living. But from this day forward (concerning you and our family, My Love) the way you will know that I love you is by what I do, not what I say. My actions will speak louder than any pretty words I will ever utter.”

        

And so that is precisely how I lead.

Simplicity

Buckminster Fuller (arguably the greatest inventor/humanitarian in history) once displayed his leadership style in this simple exchange:

        

        Person: Do you mind if I smoke?

        Bucky (Kindly.): Oh, I don’t mind for myself, darling, only for you.”

The individual promptly put the cigarette back in the pack.

This short tale sums up what I believe to be the penultimate of leadership.

        There is so much packed into this little exchange above. Examine the first part of the sentence, “Oh I don’t mind for myself…” Those six simple words express volumes of humility and selflessness. Usually a self-righteous ‘forward-thinker’ would be quick to offer a diatribe on how harmful second-hand smoke would be to their person, and how the other individual should really quit if they didn’t want to get cancer.

But not Bucky, no he is a much more subtle leader.

        The second half of the sentence: “...darling, only for you…” shows so brilliantly that a leader need only care about the needs of those under their guidance to get the best out of them.

                “No one cares how much you know, until they know how much you care.”

        There’s another layer still.

        My background is originally as a theatre actor as you may or may not know. One of my favorite directors had a way of coaching you along so that you made discovers, and you made decisions about your performance that you really cared for and wanted to perform on stage.

        What made this director so genius is that when exposed to the notes in his script, I realized that I had essentially ‘chosen’ to execute the performance he had pretty much pre-planned before we began rehearsal.

        He got me to do exactly what he needed from me, all the while giving me the satisfaction of feeling as though I had a formative role in the process--that I did it ‘by myself.’

        He got me to want to do what he needed me.

        If that ain’t leadership I don’t know what is.

Great Leadership Creates Leaders, Not Followers

        Chik-Fil-A has a wonderful model of leadership. To them, you aren’t leading well unless your people become leaders themselves. They invest in the education of their staff regardless of position, and expect them to behave as if they were board members serving the public. Observe their website and you will see their strong belief in leadership as service as opposed to leadership by commanding.

Leaders’ actions (and subsequently the actions they want their followers to take) should all be geared toward improving others’ lives. Whether it’s the lives of our customers, or the personal lives of the employees under our guidance, all we do as leaders should make life a little bit easier for someone else...even if that means you have to carry the brunt of the burden.

On The Floor, In the Trenches

        The day flew by.

        I was in what Csikszentmihalyi would call Flow.

        I was well rested, my answers were on point, and we smashed our numbers for Hyundai Swag (an exciting game show sponsored by Hyundai, you might have heard of it I’m sure). I felt so good that I stayed out on the floor (I am allowed a bit of a break between shows at Majors) just to express my excitement about the Hyundai Nexo to the skeptical but intrigued public of New York City. The day seemed to be over too soon.

The highlight was being reunited on the show with a couple who had done the game show two years earlier. We expressed our joy of the memory (thanks to Instagram) and marveled over how time passed so quickly. People remembered me, sought me, and wanted to come thank me for their experiences at the Hyundai display.

That was this past April. Bar none--best day ever.

My father-in-law lost to cancer this year. He was my best friend. I literally wanted to die, but I went to work instead. I made people smile despite wanting to scream and run to the bathroom and cry the day away. I couldn’t make it to the service in Detroit because I was on duty for Hyundai down south.

The show must go on, right?

That was the hardest auto show I ever endured. But I knew the best way I could honor my Pops was to work to the best of my ability. He was a working man, and he would have kicked my ass if he knew I didn’t go to work on the count of him. My job was to take care of my family, as he took care of his, no questions asked.

I didn’t make a big deal about it, because I teach my sons that you leave your issues at the door when you go to work.

 

Worst day ever…

…but I did my job.

I think Pops would be pleased.

I’m Not Trying to Be Rude But…

        Clumping. Like the word moist, it has become a word I loathe to hear. I hate to see it as well.

        One of my favorite tactics when I see people clumping is to engage a customer near the offenders and say:

        ME: Hello welcome to Hyundai, my name’s Eddy, but if you have any questions about this vehicle ask <insert Clumper here> they’re smarter than me.

I say this loud of enough for Clumper to hear.

I learned this technique from Bucky. (See Simplicity above).

This is especially helpful with a friend on the floor. I’ve charmed the offender with a compliment. I’ve humbled myself and taken away any sense of a threat.  I’ve made a joke to engage the customer. The Clumper is forced into doing their job because ‘they are smarter than me’ after all and now they have to prove it. Win-win-win. Yay.

Candor

        As I consider the prospect of being a TL there are still a few reservations. Most of it stems from my being the SWAG host. Never have I had a job that took full advantage of who I am as a person, performer, and information monger than Hyundai SWAG. I fear leaving SWAG to be a TL would be off somehow.

        

        When I think of the ideal TL, I envision going to an auto show (as a PS) and not being aware that anything is even wrong. The great TLs are so quick at anticipating problems and solving them that I hardly notice any issues. My attention to detail would have to take on a incredible upgrade to achieve such prowess and ability.

        I’ve been an assistant director of a theatre, managed numerous retail stores, founded and ran an entertainment company, and am currently serving my long tenure as a father. Adversity, bad behavior discussions, disappointment, and improvisation out of issues are all part of my DNA now. My ONLY reservation about all of this is simply:

Will I enjoy it as much as SWAG?

Will I do my best? Of course.

Can I do the job? I truly believe so!

Can I glean from my mentors who are doing great work already? That’s what I do.

Can I encourage others to approach the work with my same zeal and enthusiasm?

I hope so.

My greatest concern in getting joy out of the experience completely. Moreover, I feel like my style of ‘leadership’ works best when I am not a ‘leader’. Sometimes you just need somebody who is part of the team to do it right, and hope that it rubs off on everyone else. I believe (at least I truly hope) that I am that PS. I strive to be the PS that other PS look up to and say to themselves: “That’s how you do the job!”
